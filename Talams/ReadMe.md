kaarvai and Gati in carnatic music. Are they related?

Yes, kaarvai and gati (also known as nadai) are related concepts in Carnatic music as both are essential elements of its rhythmic system (tala). They interact to create the intricate rhythmic patterns characteristic of the style. [1, 2, 3, 4]  
Kaarvai 
A kaarvai refers to the sustaining or prolongation of a single musical note (swara). The duration of this pause is not arbitrary; it is a precise number of time units (matras) relative to the prevailing beat (tala). The skillful use of kaarvai is crucial for artistic expression in a raga performance and ensures that the sustained note fits correctly within the rhythmic cycle. [5, 6, 7]  
Gati (Nadai) 
Gati (or nadai in Tamil) refers to the subdivision of the beat or pulse within a specific time cycle. There are five primary gatis, each assigning a specific numerical value to the number of sub-beats per main beat: 

• Tisram (3 sub-beats) 
• Chatusram (4 sub-beats) 
• Khandam (5 sub-beats) 
• Misram (7 sub-beats) 
• Sankirnam (9 sub-beats) [11, 12]  

The Relationship 
The relationship lies in how the duration of a kaarvai is measured and executed. The exact count or number of sub-beats that a note is sustained during a kaarvai is determined by the specific gati being used in that piece or section. [13, 14]  
For example, a kaarvai in Chatusra gati (4 sub-beats per beat) would be a multiple of 4 units, while the same length of time in Tisra gati (3 sub-beats per beat) would be a multiple of 3 units. A performer must precisely time the sustained note according to the active gati to maintain rhythmic integrity within the complex tala system. [11, 15]
